The History of Sultan's Palace

Joseph Gardette was the man who had local architects build him the three and a half story mansion. After a few years he sold it to Jean LePrete and bought it for $20,049 in 1839. LePrete was incredibly wealthy and added all the iron to the balcony in the 1850's. When the Civil War erupted in the 1860's the LePrete family financial stability crumbled. They couldn't maintain the mansion anymore due to the economic downturn from the war. LePrete was desperate to get ride of the mansion so he leased his mansion in the French Quarter. He was randomly approached by a man and said that LePrete's brother that was a Sultan wanted to rent the Property. He agreed and signed all the contracts. A month later a ship arrives in the port of New Orlean's, women, men in a military uniforms, the brother of Sultan, and Sultan himself were pouring out of the boat. There was also a whole bunch of fine furniture pieces pouring out. It was then named the Sultan's Palace. The Palace was then filled with activity everyday and night.



Why is it Haunted?

A couple months after they moved in a very bad storm rolled in one night. The storm caused the whole city to hunker down and board up all there windows. The next morning the sky was bright blue as if the storm never happened. A man who was on a walk walked past the Palace and noticed blood dripping down the front steps. He ran to the police, as the police arrived the blood was even thicker. The police knocked the door down and there was corpses all over the ground, limbs were missing and some flayed open. In the courtyard they found Sultan buried, with one hand out of the dirt clawing for help. The police never found out who did it or why someone would do such a thing.
